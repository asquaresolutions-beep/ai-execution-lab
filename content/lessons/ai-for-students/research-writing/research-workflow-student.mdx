AI can cut hours off research — but only at specific stages. Used wrong, it produces a fluent summary of sources you never opened, with citations you can't defend. Used right, it accelerates discovery and synthesis while you keep control of what's actually true and actually cited.

This is the end-to-end workflow.

---

## Core principle: AI for discovery and synthesis, primary sources for truth

Split research into stages and assign each to the right tool:

- **AI is good at:** suggesting directions, framing questions, explaining unfamiliar areas, surfacing search terms, and helping you synthesise notes *you* took.
- **AI is not a source.** Every fact, quote, and citation in your final work comes from a primary source you opened and read — not from the chatbot's summary.

Hold that line and the rest of the workflow is safe.

## The five-stage pipeline

<WorkflowBlock>
<WorkflowStep number={1} title="Scope — frame the question">
Use AI to sharpen a vague topic into answerable questions and identify subtopics and key terms. This sets your search vocabulary.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={2} title="Discover — find directions and sources">
Use AI / Perplexity to surface seminal works, key authors, and search terms. Treat every suggested source as a lead to verify, never as a citation.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={3} title="Retrieve — go to the primary sources">
Find the actual papers/books via Google Scholar and your library. Save each into a reference manager immediately so the citation is captured correctly.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={4} title="Read — extract in your own words">
Read the real sources. Take notes yourself. Optionally use AI to explain a dense passage you've already read — but the notes are yours.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={5} title="Synthesise — connect your notes">
Feed your own notes to AI to find themes, tensions, and gaps. You write the synthesis; AI helps you see the structure.
</WorkflowStep>
</WorkflowBlock>

## Stage prompts

**Scope:**
<PromptBlock title="Scope the topic" role="user">
I'm researching [topic] for a [level] [essay/report] of about [word count]. Help me narrow it into 2–3 focused, answerable research questions. For each, list the key concepts and the search terms I should use to find academic sources. Don't cite sources yet — just help me frame the questions.
</PromptBlock>

**Discover (then verify everything):**
<PromptBlock title="Find directions" role="user">
For the question "[your question]", what are the major schools of thought, the most-cited foundational works, and the key authors I should look for? Present this as leads for me to verify myself — I will find and read the actual sources.
</PromptBlock>

**Synthesise (from your own notes only):**
<PromptBlock title="Synthesise my notes" role="user">
Here are my notes from the sources I've read: [paste your notes, with which source each point came from]. Identify the main themes, where sources agree or disagree, and any gap in the evidence. Do not add facts or sources that aren't in my notes.
</PromptBlock>

The constraint in that last prompt — "do not add facts or sources that aren't in my notes" — is what stops AI from fabricating into your synthesis.

## Real example

A student researching urban heat islands:
1. **Scope:** AI helps turn "cities and heat" into "How do green roofs affect surface temperature in dense cities?" plus search terms (albedo, evapotranspiration, UHI).
2. **Discover:** Perplexity surfaces a few frequently-cited authors and review papers — all treated as leads.
3. **Retrieve:** The student finds those reviews on Google Scholar, confirms they exist, saves them to Zotero.
4. **Read:** They read the reviews, taking their own notes with page references.
5. **Synthesise:** They paste *their notes* to AI, which highlights that two sources disagree on cost-effectiveness — a tension the student builds their argument around.

Every citation is real and read; AI accelerated the slow parts without becoming the source.

## Common mistakes

- **Citing AI's summary instead of the source.** If you didn't open it, you can't cite it.
- **Skipping the retrieve step.** Going straight from "AI told me" to "in my essay" is how fake citations get in.
- **Letting AI synthesise sources you never read.** Synthesis must be built on your own notes.
- **Not capturing references as you go.** Reconstructing citations later introduces errors — save to a manager at retrieval.
- **One-and-done searching.** Use AI's suggested terms to run several real searches, not to replace searching.

---

## Recommended tools

- **Perplexity** — discovery with linked sources (still open the sources).
- **Google Scholar** — find and confirm primary sources; "Cite" gives a starting reference.
- **Zotero** (free) — capture sources and generate bibliographies; the browser connector saves references in one click.
- **Claude / ChatGPT** — scoping and synthesising your own notes.
