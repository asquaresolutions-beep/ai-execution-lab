There are two ways to use AI for writing. One is to have it write your essay — fast, hollow, often against the rules, and it teaches you nothing. The other is to use it as a sharp, tireless feedback partner that makes *your* writing better. This lesson is the second one.

The principle: **AI critiques and questions; you compose and decide.**

---

## Core concept: the writing is the thinking

In academic work, composing a sentence is how you work out what you think. If AI composes for you, you skip the reasoning the assignment is meant to develop — and you end up with text you can't defend in a discussion or exam. Worse, most institutions treat AI-written submissions as misconduct.

Using AI as a partner keeps the cognitive work with you while removing the parts that don't build skill: spotting your own unclear sentences, finding the gap in your argument, catching repetition you've gone blind to.

---

## Where AI helps writing (and where it shouldn't)

**Helps — keep doing these:**
- **Before:** pressure-test your thesis; brainstorm counter-arguments; turn your messy thoughts into an outline you then refine.
- **During:** unstick you on structure ("how might I transition between these two points?") while you write the actual prose.
- **After:** critique a draft *you wrote* — clarity, logic, flow, repetition; explain a grammar rule; suggest where evidence is thin.

**Don't — these cross into ghost-writing:**
- Generating paragraphs you paste in as your own.
- Asking it to "write the essay" or "write the conclusion."
- Rewriting your sentences wholesale so the words are its, not yours.

A reliable test: **after the AI step, are the sentences in your submission yours?** If AI wrote them, it's a ghost, not a partner.

## The partner loop

<WorkflowBlock>
<WorkflowStep number={1} title="You outline">
Write your own thesis and outline. Optionally ask AI to challenge the thesis or surface a counter-argument — then you revise the outline.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={2} title="You draft">
Write the actual prose yourself. This is the non-negotiable step — the words are yours.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={3} title="AI critiques (not rewrites)">
Paste your draft and ask for specific feedback: unclear sentences, weak logic, repetition, thin evidence. Explicitly tell it not to rewrite.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={4} title="You revise">
Act on the feedback in your own words. You decide what to accept and reject.
</WorkflowStep>
<WorkflowStep number={5} title="Repeat on the weak spots">
Run another critique pass on the sections that needed the most work.
</WorkflowStep>
</WorkflowBlock>

## Prompts that get feedback, not ghost-writing

<PromptBlock title="Critique without rewriting" role="user">
Here is a paragraph I wrote: [paste].
Give me specific feedback only — do NOT rewrite it for me.
Point out: (1) any sentence that's unclear, (2) the weakest part of the argument, (3) anything repetitive. For each, tell me what to fix so I can rewrite it myself.
</PromptBlock>

<PromptBlock title="Pressure-test the argument" role="user">
My thesis is: [thesis]. Here's my outline: [outline].
What's the strongest counter-argument I'm not addressing, and where in the structure is my reasoning weakest? Don't fix it — just show me what to strengthen.
</PromptBlock>

<PromptBlock title="Explain, so you learn the rule" role="user">
In this sentence I'm unsure about the grammar / punctuation: [sentence]. Explain the rule that applies so I can fix it myself and get it right next time. Don't just give me the corrected sentence.
</PromptBlock>

The recurring instruction — *"don't rewrite it, tell me what to fix"* — is what keeps the words yours.

## Real example

A student writes a body paragraph, then runs the critique prompt. The feedback: "Sentences 2 and 3 make the same point; your evidence in sentence 4 doesn't clearly support the claim in sentence 1." The student merges the redundant sentences and adds a linking clause to connect evidence to claim — in their own words. The paragraph is now tighter and the argument clearer, and every sentence is still theirs. They also learned a pattern (claim → evidence → link) they'll reuse.

Contrast: the ghost version pastes an AI-written paragraph, reads well, but the student can't explain why it's structured that way and risks a misconduct finding.

## Academic integrity (read your specific policy)

- **Rules vary** by institution, course, and assignment — check yours. Some allow AI for feedback/brainstorming but not generation; some require a disclosure statement.
- **Disclose when required.** Many courses now ask you to state how you used AI. A short, honest note is standard practice.
- **"Partner" use is defensible; "ghost" use usually isn't.** If you wrote every sentence and used AI to critique, you can explain and defend your work — which is the real test.
- The next lesson, **Citation Integrity**, covers how to cite AI use correctly.

## Common mistakes

- **Asking it to "improve" a paragraph** and pasting the result — that's it writing, not you. Ask *what* to improve.
- **Letting your voice flatten** into generic AI prose. Keep your phrasing; act on feedback yourself.
- **Skipping your own draft** and "editing" an AI draft — the thinking never happened.
- **Ignoring your course's AI policy** — defensible workflow still has to match the stated rules.
- **Over-editing to a bland average.** Some "imperfect" voice is yours and worth keeping.

## Recommended tools

- **Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini** — for critique, counter-arguments, and explaining rules.
- A **word processor with version history** (Google Docs, Word) — shows your drafting process, useful evidence of authorship.
- Your **institution's academic-integrity / AI-use policy** — the authoritative rulebook.
